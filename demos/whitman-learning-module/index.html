The Make vs. Buy Decision

An interactive exploration of one of supply chain management's most critical strategic decisions.

1

The Framework

▼

Every product a company sells contains components. For each one, leadership faces a fundamental question: should we make it ourselves, or buy it from someone else?

Make means investing in the capability to produce a component internally: equipment, labor, facilities, and expertise.

Buy means sourcing the component from an external supplier, trading control for flexibility.

This isn't just a cost calculation. It's a strategic decision that shapes your supply chain architecture, competitive advantage, and risk profile.

Key insight: The lowest-cost option on a spreadsheet is not always the best decision. Hidden costs (coordination, quality risk, loss of IP, supply disruption) often dwarf the visible savings.

The framework balances six dimensions: cost, quality control, speed to market, strategic importance, scalability, and risk exposure. We'll explore each interactively.

Case Study: Boeing 787 Dreamliner

▼

The Boeing 787 Dreamliner is arguably the most studied make-vs-buy failure in modern supply chain history. It's a cautionary tale of what happens when outsourcing goes too far.

The bet: Boeing outsourced 60-70% of the 787's design and manufacturing to 50+ global suppliers. Historically, Boeing had been vertically integrated, building most of its aircraft in-house. The 787 broke that model completely.
2004 - The Decision
Boeing commits to radical outsourcing strategy. Partners would deliver completed modules that Boeing could "snap together" in just 3 days. Goal: cut costs, share financial risk, shorten development.
2007 - The Rollout
First 787 rolled out to fanfare. Behind the scenes: a hollow shell with plywood doors, no functional systems. Later dubbed the "fake 787." Boeing disassembled it immediately after the event.
2007-2008 - Cascading Failures
Fuselage barrels arrived without wiring, brackets, or hydraulics. A shortage of fasteners meant early planes were held together with temporary bolts from hardware stores. Sub-tier supplier problems were invisible to Boeing.
2008-2009 - Buying Back Control
Boeing acquired struggling supplier Vought Aircraft Industries' South Carolina facility for $1B, sent hundreds of engineers to supplier factories. Admission from leadership: "We outsourced too much."
2011 - First Delivery
Three years late. Billions in cost overruns. The program ultimately cost an estimated $32 billion, far exceeding the original $10B budget.

The lesson: Boeing's CFO later stated, "We gave too much work to people that had never really done this before." The cost savings on paper were real. The coordination costs, quality failures, and loss of integration expertise were not on the spreadsheet. Scott Carson, head of Boeing Commercial Airplanes, put it simply: "In addition to oversight, you need insight into what's actually going on in those factories."
